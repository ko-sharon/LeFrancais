## 💡 Key Grammar Notes

- **Conditionnel passé** (*aurait + participe passé*): used for hypothetical past situations — *Si tu t'étais levée, je t'aurais appelée* (If you had gotten up, I would have called you). This is the **plus-que-parfait + conditionnel passé** construction.

- **Plus-que-parfait**: *s'était levée*, *avait été*, *n'avaient pas le temps* — used for an action completed **before another past action**.

- **Agreement of past participle with direct object**: When a direct object pronoun precedes the verb, the past participle agrees in gender and number: *Il les a vues* (les vaches → vues); *Il l'a vue* (la vache → vue); *Elle l'a habillée* (la sœur → habillée); *Il les a vus* (les cochons → vus).

- **Pronoms *y* et *en***: *y* replaces a place (*à la ferme*, *à Londres*); *en* replaces a quantity or a noun preceded by *de* (*des vaches* → *en*). When both are needed together: *il y en a beaucoup*.

- **Pronoms *qui* vs *que*** in relative clauses: *qui* = subject of the relative clause; *que* = direct object. When *que* precedes a past participle, agreement applies: *la vache qu'il a vue*.

- **Pronoms personnels compléments**: direct objects (*le, la, les*) vs indirect objects (*lui, leur*). With verbs like *téléphoner à*, *donner à*, *dire à*, *demander à* — the person is an **indirect object** → use *lui* (singular) or *leur* (plural). With *laver, appeler, habiller* — the person is a **direct object** → use *le, la, les*.

- **sauf** = except: *Tous sauf Henri* (Everyone except Henri) — equivalent to *excepté*.

- **pendant que** = while (simultaneous actions): *pendant que les autres dorment* — contrasted with *quand* which can imply sequence.

- **Futur proche vs futur simple**: *nous allons faire une promenade* (immediate future, informal) vs *nous irons à la ferme* (future tense, more formal or further in time).
