# French Guide: Tricky Concepts from Chapitre 7

## Concept 2 — *des* vs *de*

### The five jobs of *des* and *de*

This one trips up almost every English speaker because English uses one word — "of" or "some" — loosely. French is more precise.

---

### Job A — *de* = of (possession / relationship)

When linking two nouns to show **belonging or relationship**, use *de*. 

| French | English |
|---|---|
| Le nom de famille de M. Duclos | The family name of Mr. Duclos |
| La maison de mon père | My father's house |
| Le frère de ta mère | Your mother's brother |
| Les enfants de M. et Mme Duclos | The children of Mr. and Mrs. Duclos |

---

### Job B — *des* = some 

When you talk about a **plural thing in general**, without specifying exactly which ones, you use *des*. It is the plural of *un/une*. 

> Indefinite Plural (IP)
These are things that are **countable**, but you're not specifying how many (example: some ideas, some shoes, etc.)
> Partitive Plural (PP)
These are things that are **uncountable**, often abstract (example: information)

| French | English |
|---|---|
| Il y a des arbres devant la maison. | IP: There are (some) trees in front of the house. |
| Elle mange des poires. | IP: She is eating (some) pears. |
| Les enfants ont des cadeaux. | IP: The children have (some) gifts. |
| J'ai des amis à Paris. | IP: I have (some) friends in Paris. |
| Il a reçu des renseignements. | PP: He got some information. |

> In English, "some" is often dropped. In French, *des* cannot be dropped in this role.

---

### Job C — *des* reduces to *de* with adjective or negation

> When **des** (from Job B) is used in front of an adjective, the *des* collapses into *de*.

#### After quantity expressions:

| French | English |
|---|---|
| beaucoup **de** fruits | a lot of fruit |
| peu **d**'arbres | few trees |
| assez **de** vin | enough wine |
| trop **de** chocolat | too much chocolate |


> When **des** (from Job B) is used in a negative construction, the *des* collapses into *de*.

#### After negation:

| Positive | Negative |
|---|---|
| Il y a **des** arbres. | Il n'y a pas **d**'arbres. |
| Elle mange **des** carottes. | Elle ne mange pas **de** carottes. |
| Il a **des** amis. | Il n'a pas **d**'amis. |

---

### Job D — *de ... à ...* = from ... to ...

This use of *de* marks a **range** — in time, distance, or sequence. It always pairs with *à*.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| **de** huit heures **à** midi | from eight o'clock to noon |
| **de** Paris **à** Marseille | from Paris to Marseille |
| **de** janvier **à** mars | from January to March |
| **de** la maison **à** l'école | from the house to the school |
| *du* lundi *au* vendredi | from Monday to Friday |

> **Note:** *de le* contracts to **du**, and *de les* contracts to **des**.  
> So *de le lundi* → **du lundi**, and *de les enfants* → **des enfants**.  
> This is why *des* sometimes looks like Job B ("some") but is actually a contraction. Context tells you which it is.

---

### Job E — *des* + noun + *où* (days/places "when/where")

When *des* introduces a plural noun referring to **time or place**, and you want to add a clause describing it, French uses *où* — not *que*, not *quand*.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| En hiver, il y a des jours **où** la neige tombe. | In winter, there are days **when** snow falls. |
| Il y a des moments **où** je ne comprends pas. | There are moments **when** I don't understand. |
| Il y a des endroits **où** il fait très froid. | There are places **where** it is very cold. |
| Il y a des saisons **où** les enfants ne vont pas à l'école. | There are seasons **when** children don't go to school. |

> **Why *où* and not *que*?**  
> *Que* replaces a direct object. But *jours, moments, endroits* here are not objects — they are time or place references inside the second clause. French uses *où* for both "when" and "where" in relative clauses. Think of it as the relative pronoun for time and place, just as *que* is the relative pronoun for objects.

> **Why *des* and not *de*?**  
> There is no negation and no quantity word here, so *des* stays as *des*. Compare:  
> - *Il y a **des** jours où il neige.* → There are days when it snows. (*des* = plain plural)  
> - *Il y a peu **de** jours où il neige.* → There are few days when it snows. (*peu* = quantity word, so *de*)

---

### Quick summary table

| Situation | Use |
|---|---|
| Plural "some" (positive sentence) | *des* |
| After ne...pas / ne...plus | *de* (or *d'* before vowel) |
| After beaucoup, peu, assez, trop... | *de* (or *d'*) |
| Possession / relationship between nouns | *de* |
| Range: from ... to ... | *de ... à ...* |
| Plural time/place noun + relative clause | *des ... où* |

### Drills — Translate into French

1. There are some dogs in the house.
2. There are not any dogs in the house.
3. Mr. Duclos's garden is beautiful.
4. She eats a lot of fruit.
5. Do you have any friends in France?
6. He does not have any friends in France.
7. A glass of red wine, please.
8. There are few trees in front of the school.

**Answers:**
1. Il y a des chiens dans la maison.
2. Il n'y a pas de chiens dans la maison.
3. Le jardin de M. Duclos est beau.
4. Elle mange beaucoup de fruits.
5. Avez-vous des amis en France?
6. Il n'a pas d'amis en France.
7. Un verre de vin rouge, s'il vous plaît.
8. Il y a peu d'arbres devant l'école.
