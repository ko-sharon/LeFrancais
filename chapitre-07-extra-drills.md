## Concept 1 — *il y a* vs *il a*

### What you need to feel

*Il y a* is a frozen, impersonal phrase. Think of it like 有 in Mandarin — it describes the **existence** of something, usually somewhere. You never split it up.

*Il a* means **he has**. There is a real person holding or owning something.

### Read these, feel the difference

| French | English |
|---|---|
| Il y a un jardin devant la maison. | There is a garden in front of the house. |
| M. Duclos a un jardin. | Mr. Duclos has a garden. |
| Il y a deux chiens dans la maison. | There are two dogs in the house. |
| Henri a deux chiens. | Henri has two dogs. |
| Il y avait beaucoup de monde. | There were a lot of people. |
| Il avait beaucoup d'amis. | He had many friends. |
| Il y aura une fête demain. | There will be a party tomorrow. |
| Il aura huit ans demain. | He will be eight years old tomorrow. |

### Notice
- *il y a* → answers "what exists here / somewhere?"
- *il a* → answers "what does he own or possess?"
- The *y* never disappears when you use this expression. *Il n'y a pas*, *il n'y avait que*, *y a-t-il?* — the *y* is always there.

## Concept 2 — *de* vs *des*

### The three jobs of *de* and *des*

This one trips up almost every English speaker because English uses one word — "of" or "some" — loosely. French is more precise.

---

### Job A — *des* = some (indefinite plural)

When you talk about a **plural thing in general**, without specifying exactly which ones, you use *des*. It is the plural of *un/une*.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| Il y a des arbres devant la maison. | There are (some) trees in front of the house. |
| Elle mange des poires. | She is eating (some) pears. |
| Les enfants ont des cadeaux. | The children have (some) gifts. |
| J'ai des amis à Paris. | I have (some) friends in Paris. |

> In English, "some" is often dropped. In French, *des* cannot be dropped in this role.

---

### Job B — *de* = of (possession / relationship)

When linking two nouns to show **belonging or relationship**, use *de*. This is like 的 in Mandarin.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| Le nom de famille de M. Duclos | The family name of Mr. Duclos |
| La maison de mon père | My father's house |
| Le frère de ta mère | Your mother's brother |
| Les enfants de M. et Mme Duclos | The children of Mr. and Mrs. Duclos |

---

### Job C — *de* replaces *des* after negation or after expressions of quantity

This is the rule that surprises people most. After **ne...pas**, **ne...plus**, **ne...que** (sometimes), or after **quantity words** like *beaucoup de, peu de, assez de*, the *des* collapses into *de*.

#### After negation:

| Positive | Negative |
|---|---|
| Il y a **des** arbres. | Il n'y a pas **d'**arbres. |
| Elle mange **des** carottes. | Elle ne mange pas **de** carottes. |
| Il a **des** amis. | Il n'a pas **d'**amis. |

#### After quantity expressions:

| French | English |
|---|---|
| beaucoup **de** fruits | a lot of fruit |
| peu **d'**arbres | few trees |
| assez **de** vin | enough wine |
| trop **de** chocolat | too much chocolate |
| un verre **de** vin | a glass of wine |
| une tasse **de** café | a cup of coffee |

> Notice: *peu d'arbres devant la maison* — this is exactly your sentence from the question! *Peu* is a quantity word, so *des arbres* becomes *de/d' arbres*.

---

### Job D — *de ... à ...* = from ... to ...

This use of *de* marks a **range** — in time, distance, or sequence. It always pairs with *à*.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| de huit heures à midi | from eight o'clock to noon |
| de Paris à Marseille | from Paris to Marseille |
| du lundi au vendredi | from Monday to Friday |
| de janvier à mars | from January to March |
| de la maison à l'école | from the house to the school |

> **Note:** *de le* contracts to **du**, and *de les* contracts to **des**.  
> So *de le lundi* → **du lundi**, and *de les enfants* → **des enfants**.  
> This is why *des* sometimes looks like Job A ("some") but is actually a contraction. Context tells you which it is.

> **Mandarin parallel:** 从……到…… works the same way — 从巴黎到马赛 = *de Paris à Marseille*. The logic is identical.

---

### Job E — *des* + noun + *où* (days/places "when/where")

When *des* introduces a plural noun referring to **time or place**, and you want to add a clause describing it, French uses *où* — not *que*, not *quand*.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| En hiver, il y a des jours **où** la neige tombe. | In winter, there are days **when** snow falls. |
| Il y a des moments **où** je ne comprends pas. | There are moments **when** I don't understand. |
| Il y a des endroits **où** il fait très froid. | There are places **where** it is very cold. |
| Il y a des saisons **où** les enfants ne vont pas à l'école. | There are seasons **when** children don't go to school. |

> **Why *où* and not *que*?**  
> *Que* replaces a direct object. But *jours, moments, endroits* here are not objects — they are time or place references inside the second clause. French uses *où* for both "when" and "where" in relative clauses. Think of it as the relative pronoun for time and place, just as *que* is the relative pronoun for objects.

> **Why *des* and not *de*?**  
> There is no negation and no quantity word here, so *des* stays as *des*. Compare:  
> - *Il y a **des** jours où il neige.* → There are days when it snows. (*des* = plain plural)  
> - *Il y a peu **de** jours où il neige.* → There are few days when it snows. (*peu* = quantity word, so *de*)

---

### Quick summary table

| Situation | Use |
|---|---|
| Plural "some" (positive sentence) | *des* |
| After ne...pas / ne...plus | *de* (or *d'* before vowel) |
| After beaucoup, peu, assez, trop... | *de* (or *d'*) |
| Possession / relationship between nouns | *de* |
| Range: from ... to ... | *de ... à ...* |
| Plural time/place noun + relative clause | *des ... où* |

## Concept 3 — *que* (two very different jobs)

*Que* is one of those small French words that does two separate jobs. Once you see them clearly, you will not confuse them.

---

### Job 1 — *que* as a question word = "what?"

When *que* starts a question, it means **what**. It replaces the object of the verb.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| **Que** manges-tu? | What are you eating? |
| **Que** boit-il? | What is he drinking? |
| **Que** fait Amélie? | What is Amélie doing? |
| **Qu'**est-ce que tu veux? | What do you want? |

> *Que* shortens to *qu'* before a vowel: *Qu'est-ce que*, *qu'a-t-il dit?*

---

### Job 2 — *que* as a relative pronoun = "that / which / whom"

When *que* appears in the **middle** of a sentence connecting two parts, it is a relative pronoun. It refers back to a noun just mentioned (its antecedent), and it stands in for the **object** of the second verb.

| French | English |
|---|---|
| la poire **que** tu manges | the pear that you are eating |
| le chien **que** j'ai vu | the dog that I saw |
| la lettre **qu'**il a écrite | the letter that he wrote |
| les cadeaux **que** les enfants ont donnés | the gifts that the children gave |

#### How to spot it:
- Question *que*: sits at the **start** of a question. → *Que manges-tu?*
- Relative *que*: sits **after a noun**, connecting two clauses. → *la poire **que** tu manges*

#### A useful Mandarin comparison:
Relative *que* works similarly to 的 in a relative clause:
- 你吃的梨 = la poire que tu manges (the pear that you are eating)

The key difference: in French, *que* comes **after** the noun it describes, not before.

### Read these pairs together

| Question que | Relative que |
|---|---|
| Que manges-tu? | la poire que tu manges |
| Que boit-il? | le vin qu'il boit |
| Que cherche-t-il? | la clef qu'il cherche |
| Qu'a-t-il dit? | les mots qu'il a dits |
| Que fait-elle? | la chose qu'elle fait |

Fill in the blank with the correct word or expression (*il y a / il a / de / des / que / qu'*).

1. _______ beaucoup _______ monde dans la rue aujourd'hui.
2. Jean _______ un ami _______ demeure à Paris.
3. _______ mange-t-elle au déjeuner?
4. Il n'y a pas _______ vin dans son verre.
5. La poire _______ tu manges vient du jardin _______ mon père.
6. _______ t-il _______ enfants, M. Lebrun?
7. Nous n'avons pas _______ amis ici.
8. _______ dit M. Duclos à sa femme?

10. Il y a peu _______ fleurs, mais _______ beaux arbres dans ce jardin.

**Answers:**
1. Il y a / de
2. a / qui *(note: qui, not que — see below)*
3. Que
4. de
5. que / de
6. Y a / des
7. d'
8. Que
9. qu' / de

> **Note on answer 2:** *un ami qui demeure à Paris* uses **qui** (not *que*) because the friend is the **subject** of *demeure*. *Qui* = subject relative pronoun. *Que* = object relative pronoun. This distinction will come up naturally as you read more — don't worry about it yet, just notice the pattern.
